Choosing where to build your career is one of the most consequential decisions a professional can make. For many of the candidates we work with, the choice comes down to two well-trodden paths: the **H-1B visa** in the United States and **Express Entry** in Canada. Both can lead to a rewarding full-time career — but they work very differently.

## The H-1B route

The H-1B is an employer-sponsored work visa. That single fact shapes everything about it.

- **You need a job offer first.** An employer must petition on your behalf.
- **It is capped and lottery-based.** Demand routinely exceeds the annual quota, so selection is partly down to chance.
- **It is tied to your employer.** Changing jobs means transferring the visa.

The cap stands at 65,000 visas per fiscal year, with an additional 20,000 reserved for candidates holding a US master's degree or higher. When applications exceed the cap — which they consistently do — USCIS runs a random lottery among eligible petitions. Employers typically file in April for an October start date, meaning even a successful lottery draw can take six months or more before your first day.

The upside is significant: the US technology market is the largest in the world, compensation is high, and the H-1B can be a stepping stone toward permanent residency via employment-based green card categories (EB-1 through EB-3). That path has its own queue — sometimes measured in years — but the H-1B keeps you legally employed while you wait.

A few nuances worth knowing:

- **Transfers are permitted.** If you change employers while on H-1B, your new employer files a transfer petition. You can usually start working before the transfer is approved.
- **STEM OPT extends your runway.** If you recently graduated from a US university with a STEM degree, Optional Practical Training (OPT) gives you up to three years of work authorization, overlapping with H-1B lottery cycles.
- **Large tech companies have more capacity.** Firms that file high volumes of H-1B petitions have experience navigating the process and often have in-house immigration counsel, which reduces friction for you.

## The Express Entry route

Express Entry is Canada's points-based system for skilled workers. Rather than starting with an employer, you start with your own profile.

- **You apply directly.** No job offer is strictly required to enter the pool.
- **It is merit-ranked.** Your age, education, language scores, and experience produce a score that determines your competitiveness.
- **It leads to permanent residency.** Successful candidates are invited to apply for PR, not a temporary visa.

The three main immigration streams under Express Entry are the Federal Skilled Worker Program, the Federal Skilled Trades Program, and the Canadian Experience Class. Each has its own eligibility criteria, but all flow through the same Comprehensive Ranking System (CRS) points pool.

Your CRS score is calculated from factors including:
- **Language proficiency** in English and/or French (IELTS or CELPIP for English, TEF for French)
- **Age** — points peak in the mid-twenties and taper gradually
- **Level of education** — an ECA (Educational Credential Assessment) is required for international degrees
- **Paid work experience** — both inside and outside Canada
- **Adaptability factors** — such as a Canadian job offer or a sibling who is a permanent resident

IRCC runs draws every two weeks, inviting candidates above a score threshold to apply for PR. Cut-off scores fluctuate with demand, but strong profiles — particularly those with Canadian work experience, high language scores, or a job offer from a designated employer — tend to fare well.

Processing time for a complete PR application is typically six months or less, which is notably faster than many US green card pathways.

> The fundamental difference: H-1B is *employer-first*, Express Entry is *candidate-first*.

## Timing and realistic timelines

Understanding the clock on each route helps you plan:

| | H-1B | Express Entry |
|---|---|---|
| Starting point | Job offer in hand | Profile submission |
| Key waiting period | Lottery (April) → October start | Time in the pool until draw |
| End state | Temporary work status (initially 3 years) | Permanent residency |
| Path to permanence | Employment-based green card (EB-1 to EB-3) | Already built in |

The H-1B timeline depends heavily on the lottery and employer readiness. Express Entry draws happen on a roughly fortnightly cycle, so a strong profile can move from submission to invitation within weeks to months.

## How to choose

Ask yourself three questions:

1. **Do you already have, or can you realistically secure, a US employer willing to sponsor?** If yes, the H-1B becomes viable.
2. **How much certainty do you need?** Express Entry rewards strong profiles predictably; the H-1B lottery introduces uncertainty.
3. **What is your long-term goal — speed of entry, or permanence?**

Candidates with high CRS scores, strong English proficiency, and relevant Canadian work experience are particularly well-positioned for Express Entry. Candidates with strong US employer relationships in tech, finance, or engineering often find the H-1B worth pursuing despite the lottery — especially if they have a master's degree that enters them into the separate 20,000-cap pool.

There is no universally correct answer. The right path is the one that matches your profile, your timeline, and your appetite for risk.

## Common misconceptions

**"The H-1B is the only way to work in the US."** Not true. TN visas (for Canadian and Mexican nationals under the USMCA), L-1 intracompany transfers, and O-1 visas for individuals with extraordinary ability each serve different candidate profiles.

**"Express Entry is a long process."** For well-qualified candidates, the time from profile creation to receiving an Invitation to Apply can be shorter than completing a single US hiring cycle. PR processing itself is typically faster than an employment-based US green card.

**"You have to choose one and commit."** Many candidates run both tracks simultaneously — building their CRS profile while pursuing US roles — and let whichever moves first determine the destination.
