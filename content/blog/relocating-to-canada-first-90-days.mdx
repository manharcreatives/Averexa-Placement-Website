You signed the offer. Congratulations — and now the real logistics begin. The first ninety days in a new country set the tone for everything that follows. Here is a grounded, sequenced guide for professionals relocating to Canada.

## Province matters: choosing your home base

Most professionals moving for a specific job do not choose their province — the job chooses it for them. But if you have flexibility, it is worth understanding the landscape:

- **Ontario (Toronto and the GTA)** is Canada's largest tech hub, with the broadest range of employers and the most active recruitment market. Cost of living, particularly housing, is high.
- **British Columbia (Vancouver)** offers a strong tech presence, proximity to the US Pacific Northwest ecosystem, and a mild climate. Also high cost of living.
- **Alberta (Calgary and Edmonton)** is more affordable and growing, particularly in energy technology, fintech, and engineering. No provincial income tax, which improves net take-home.
- **Quebec (Montreal)** is a strong AI and gaming hub with a lower cost of living and a vibrant cultural scene, but French proficiency is an advantage and sometimes required.

Each province also has its own Provincial Nominee Program (PNP) which can create a faster Express Entry pathway if you are already working there.

## Days 1–7: The essentials

Get the foundations in place immediately, because everything else depends on them.

- **Social Insurance Number (SIN)** — required to work and get paid. Apply in person at Service Canada or online. Bring your work permit or PR confirmation.
- **Bank account** — most major Canadian banks (RBC, TD, BMO, Scotiabank, CIBC) offer newcomer packages with waived fees for the first year. Bring your passport, work permit, and a utility bill or lease if you have one.
- **Phone plan** — a local number unlocks nearly every other service. Prepaid is fine to start; you can move to a post-paid plan once you have credit history.
- **Temporary housing** — secure short-term accommodation before committing to a lease. Extended-stay hotels, furnished suites, and short-term rental platforms give you time to understand neighbourhoods before signing a twelve-month commitment.

Do these in roughly this order. The SIN is the first domino — without it, nothing else in the financial system moves.

## Days 8–30: Build your base

With essentials handled, establish the systems of daily life.

1. **Permanent housing** — understand neighbourhoods, commute times, and lease norms. Canadian landlords typically require first and last month's rent plus references. As a newcomer without Canadian rental history, a letter from your employer confirming your role and salary strengthens your application significantly.
2. **Provincial health coverage** — enrollment timing varies by province. Ontario and British Columbia have a waiting period before coverage activates; Alberta does not. Check your province's rules immediately so you know whether you need interim private coverage.
3. **Transit and ID** — a provincial driver's licence (converted from your home country's licence in most cases) and a transit pass for your city. International licences are valid temporarily, but converting early simplifies day-to-day life.

> Settling in is a project. Treat it like one — with a checklist and a sequence — and the overwhelm fades quickly.

## Days 31–60: Find your footing at work

- **Learn the workplace culture.** Canadian workplaces tend to value directness balanced with politeness, and collaboration over hierarchy. Consensus-building is common before decisions are made. This can feel slower than environments where decisions are more top-down, but it tends to mean broad buy-in once a direction is set.
- **Build relationships early.** A coffee with each teammate pays off for months. Canadian professional culture is warm but reserved at first — initiative to connect is well-received and often appreciated.
- **Clarify expectations** with your manager in your first weeks rather than assuming. Canada's workplace culture generally rewards people who ask questions and seek clarity over those who guess and miss.

On the practical side: make sure your payroll is set up correctly. Review your first pay stub and confirm your tax withholding matches your expected situation. If you have multiple income sources or cross-provincial complexity, a quick consult with a Canadian accountant in your first month saves headaches at tax time.

## Days 61–90: Put down roots

- **Build a local network** — professional meetups, alumni groups, community organizations, and sector-specific events. LinkedIn Canada is active; local groups in your industry often host regular events.
- **Start building Canadian credit history.** Credit history does not transfer internationally. A secured credit card (where you deposit collateral against the credit limit) is the standard starting point. Use it regularly and pay it in full each month. After six to twelve months of responsible use, your score will be sufficient to qualify for standard cards, car financing, and eventually a mortgage.
- **Understand the TFSA and RRSP.** As a tax resident of Canada, you begin accumulating contribution room in the Tax-Free Savings Account (TFSA) immediately. Contributions to your TFSA grow tax-free, and withdrawals are also tax-free — it is one of the most valuable savings vehicles available to Canadian residents. The Registered Retirement Savings Plan (RRSP) gives you a tax deduction on contributions. Both are worth understanding in your first year, even if you start contributing in small amounts.
- **Explore.** The faster a new place feels like home, the better you perform at work.

## The professional side of settling in

Your professional identity resets somewhat when you relocate. The networks, reputation, and context you built in your home country do not travel automatically.

A few practices that accelerate re-establishment:

- **Update your LinkedIn profile** to reflect your Canadian location. Canadian recruiters and hiring managers search by location.
- **Join professional associations** in your field. Many Canadian industries have active national and provincial associations that run events, mentorship programs, and job boards.
- **Reconnect with anyone in your extended network who has Canadian connections.** Alumni from your university, colleagues who moved before you, and LinkedIn connections in Canada are all warm starting points.

Visibility matters early. Showing up — at a meetup, on a professional Slack, at an industry event — accelerates the network-building that would otherwise take years.
